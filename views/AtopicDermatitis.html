ATOPIC DERMATITIS


Definition:

Atopic dermatitis is a chronic skin disorder that is characterized by inflammation of the skin. It is considered a type of eczema and is part of a group of conditions known as atopic diseases, which also include asthma and hay fever. It often begins in childhood but can affect individuals of all ages

Cause:

The exact cause of atopic dermatitis is not fully understood, but it is believed to result from a combination of genetic, environmental, and immune system factors. Individuals with a family history of atopic diseases are more likely to develop AD.


Common Symptoms of Atopic Dermatitis:

Itching (Pruritus): Intense itching is the hallmark symptom of atopic dermatitis. The itching can be severe and may lead to scratching, which can further irritate the skin. Redness and Inflammation: Affected skin areas often become red, swollen, and inflamed. This inflammation can vary in severity. Dryness: AD can cause the skin to become extremely dry, making it prone to cracking, scaling, and peeling. Rash: A rash is a common feature of atopic dermatitis. The rash can vary in appearance and may include raised, scaly patches, bumps, or small fluid-filled blisters. Oozing and Crusting: In more severe cases, the affected skin may ooze fluid and develop crusts, especially if it has been scratched repeatedly. Skin Thickening: Over time, chronic scratching or rubbing of the affected areas can lead to thickened skin, a condition called lichenification. Skin Discoloration: Some individuals with AD may develop patches of skin that become lighter or darker than the surrounding skin. Secondary Infections: Scratching can break the skin's barrier, making it susceptible to bacterial or viral infections, which can worsen the symptoms.
